1. The ML Model (Isolation Forest)
The system is trained on 3,500 examples of human work behavior. It looks for "Anomalies" (patterns that don't fit a real human).

The Input: It takes the timing of your last 10-20 heartbeats.
The Analysis: A human moves their mouse or clicks with "Jitter" (natural imperfection). A bot script sends a heartbeat exactly every 60.000 seconds.
The Verdict: If the timing is too perfect, the ML model flags it as a bot and drops your trust score.
2. The TypeScript Fallback (Rule-Based)
If your Python ML service is offline, the backend uses a built-in Standard Deviation (Jitter) algorithm.

It calculates the Standard Deviation of your heartbeat gaps in milliseconds.
Human Jitter: ~400ms to 2000ms (High variance).
Bot Jitter: < 150ms (Low variance).

The TypeScript Fallback algorithm is the "Internal Math" the backend uses if the complex Python ML service is not reachable. It uses a statistical concept called Standard Deviation to measure the "Jitter" of the heartbeats.

Here is the step-by-step breakdown of that algorithm:

Step 1: Calculate the "Gaps"
First, the backend takes the last few heartbeats (e.g., 5 heartbeats) and measures the time (in milliseconds) between each one.

Heartbeat #1 to #2: 60,847ms
Heartbeat #2 to #3: 59,210ms
Heartbeat #3 to #4: 61,005ms
Heartbeat #4 to #5: 58,938ms
Step 2: Find the "Mean" (Average Gap)
It adds them up and divides by the count:

(60,847 + 59,210 + 61,005 + 58,938) / 4 = 60,000ms

Step 3: Calculate the "Variance" (Difference from Average)
The algorithm looks at how "far" each gap is from that 60-second average:

(60,847 - 60,000) = +847ms
(59,210 - 60,000) = -790ms
(61,005 - 60,000) = +1005ms
(58,938 - 60,000) = -1062ms
Step 4: Final "Jitter" Score (Standard Deviation)
It squares these numbers, averages them, and takes the square root. This results in the Standard Deviation (Jitter).

In this example, the Jitter would be roughly ~900ms.
Step 5: The "Human vs. Bot" Verdict
The algorithm then checks that Jitter against these fixed rules:

Jitter Value	Verdict	Logic
Below 150ms	BOT (0.1 Score)	No human can hit a timer with less than 150ms of variance consistently.
150ms – 400ms	SUSPICIOUS (0.6 Score)	This could be a very stable bot, or a human on a perfect fiber connection.
Above 400ms	HUMAN (1.0 Score)	This is the natural "Noise" of a real human's browser and internet.

1. Does the Hash Chain "Break"?
Mathematically, no. But logically, the system will split your work into two separate sessions.

Session A (Original): Lasts until you stop moving.
The 30-Minute Gap: Because you were idle for 30 minutes (which is longer than our 15-minute "Resume" window), the Backend closes Session A.
Session B (New): When you move your mouse again after 30 minutes, the Backend says: "The last session was too long ago. Let's start a Fresh Chain (Minute 1) for this new work period."
2. Can you get Paid? (The "Recency" vs "Length" Trade-off)
Whether you get paid depends on when you click the "Analyze Risk" button:

Scenario 1: You click "Analyze Risk" immediately after starting again.
The Good: Your recencyMins is very low (e.g., 1 minute). This is Perfect. ✅
The Bad: Your Active Minutes for this session is only 1 or 2 minutes.
The Result: The Pillar 3 (Work-Proof) score will be very low because you haven't "proven" your work in this specific session yet. Outcome: No Auto-Pay (likely REVIEW status). ❌
Scenario 2: You work for another 30 mins after the gap and THEN click.
The Good: You now have 30 minutes of activeMinutes in the current session.
The Result: The trust score will be much higher. Outcome: Instant Payout! ✅
3. What happens to your old 30 minutes (Session A)?
The system only uses the LATEST session to verify a claim.

Why? Because we need to be sure you were working right when the storm hit.
If you worked for 5 hours, then went to sleep for 3 hours, then claimed for a storm that just started... your old 5 hours of work doesn't "prove" you were affected by the new storm.

Final Verdict:
The chain doesn't "break," but the Insurance Window resets if you are idle for too long. To get a perfect payout, a worker should try to stay active (or at least move the mouse every 20 mins) while the storm is approaching!
